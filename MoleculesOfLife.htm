Biology
The scientific study of life.
Emergent Properties
A charactertistic of a system that does not appear in any of the system's parts.
Life's organizational levels
1) Atoms: The building block of life.
2) Molecules: Atoms joined with chemical bonds.
3) Cells: The smallest unit of life.
4) Tissues: An organized array of cells.
5) Organs: Structural unit of interacting tissues.
6) Organ System: A set of interacting organs.
7) Multicelled Organism: Individual consisting of one or more cells.
8) Population: Group of organism of a given species in an area.
9) Community: Group of populations in an area.
10) Ecosystem: Community acting with its environment (Energy flow and nutrient cycle).
11) Biosphere: All ecosystems on earth.
Energy and Nutrients
Are needed by all organisms to survive.
Producers (plants) use energy from environment and simple materials to make food, via photosynthesis.
Consumers (animals) cannot make their own food, so they gain energy and nutrients from other organisms.
Homeostasis
Organism keeping internal conditions within liveable conditions
DNA
Nucleic acid which contains hereditary information and guides the following biological processes:
1) Development: Cell of new individual becomes a multicelled adult.
2) Growth: Organism's cell size, volume, and number increases.
3) Reproduction: Producing offspring, which inherit parent's DNA.
Biodiversity
Variation among organisms.
Prokaryotes: Bacteria and Archaea, organisms that lack a nucleus.
Eukaryotes: Protists, Fungi, Plants, Animals, organisms that have a nucleus.
Taxonomy
Naming and classifying a new species. A Linnean name consists of two parts, the genus and a specific epithet. Today, we separate species by the following taxa: domain, kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species.
Critical thinking
Judging the quality of information before accepting it.
Types of Reasoning
Inductive Reasoning: Drawing a conclusion based on an observation.
Deductive Reasoning: Using a general idea to make a conclusion about a specific case.
Parts of an Experiment
Hypothesis: A testable explanation for a natural phenomenon.
Prediction: A statement of some condition that should exist if the hypothesis is correct.
Independent Variable: Variable controlled during the experiment.
Dependent Variable: Variable being observed in the experiment.
Control: Group of individuals not exposed to the variable being tested.
Sampling Error
Error caused by testing a small subset of a group that does not accurately represent the larger group.
Statistically Significant
A result that is statistically unlikely to have occured by chance.
Law of Nature
A consistent natural phenomenon for which there is incomplete scientific explanation.
Scientific Theory
Hypothesis that has not been disproven after years of rigorous testing.
