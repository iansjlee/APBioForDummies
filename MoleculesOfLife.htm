Chapter 1: Invitation to Biology
Click the black boxes to reveal the definitions.
Biology
The scientific study of life.
Emergent Properties
A charactertistic of a system that does not appear in any of the system's parts.
Life's organizational levels
1) Atoms: The building block of life.
2) Molecules: Atoms joined with chemical bonds.
3) Cells: The smallest unit of life.
4) Tissues: An organized array of cells.
5) Organs: Structural unit of interacting tissues.
6) Organ System: A set of interacting organs.
7) Multicelled Organism: Individual consisting of one or more cells.
8) Population: Group of organism of a given species in an area.
9) Community: Group of populations in an area.
10) Ecosystem: Community acting with its environment (Energy flow and nutrient cycle).
11) Biosphere: All ecosystems on earth.
Energy and Nutrients
Are needed by all organisms to survive.
Producers (plants) use energy from environment and simple materials to make food, via photosynthesis.
Consumers (animals) cannot make their own food, so they gain energy and nutrients from other organisms.
Homeostasis
Organism keeping internal conditions within liveable conditions.
DNA
Nucleic acid which contains hereditary information and guides the following biological processes:
1) Development: Cell of new individual becomes a multicelled adult.
2) Growth: Organism's cell size, volume, and number increases.
3) Reproduction: Producing offspring, which inherit parent's DNA.
Biodiversity
Variation among organisms.
Prokaryotes: Bacteria and Archaea, organisms that lack a nucleus.
Eukaryotes: Protists, Fungi, Plants, Animals, organisms that have a nucleus.
Taxonomy
Naming and classifying a new species. A Linnean name consists of two parts, the genus and a specific epithet. Today, we separate species by the following taxa: domain, kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species.
Critical thinking
Judging the quality of information before accepting it.
Types of Reasoning
Inductive Reasoning: Drawing a conclusion based on an observation.
Deductive Reasoning: Using a general idea to make a conclusion about a specific case.
Parts of an Experiment
Hypothesis: A testable explanation for a natural phenomenon.
Prediction: A statement of some condition that should exist if the hypothesis is correct.
Independent Variable: Variable controlled during the experiment.
Dependent Variable: Variable being observed in the experiment.
Control: Group of individuals not exposed to the variable being tested.
Sampling Error
Error caused by testing a small subset of a group that does not accurately represent the larger group.
Statistically Significant
A result that is statistically unlikely to have occured by chance.
Law of Nature
A consistent natural phenomenon for which there is incomplete scientific explanation.
Scientific Theory
Hypothesis that has not been disproven after years of rigorous testing.
Chapter 2: Life's Chemical Basis
Click the black boxes to reveal the definitions.
Parts of an Atom
Protons (positively charged), neutrons (no charge), electrons (negatively charged).
Element
A pure substance consisting of atoms with the same atomic number (number of protons). These elements (sorted by atomic number) make up the periodic table).
Isotope
An element that differs in the number of neutrons. They are defined by their mass number, which is the weight of all protons and neutrons.
Radioisotope
An isotope that is radioactive (nucleus breaks down spontaneously). This decay happens at a predictable rate and is not altered by external factors.
Tracer
A substance with a detectable component, such as a radioisotope. These can be easily followed in a biological system, making them useful in research.
Shell Model
Model of an atom showing electron distribution, which corresponds to energy levels.
Free Radical
Atom with an unpaired electron. Atoms are usually in this state only momentarily, as they gain or lose electrons by interacting with other atoms.
Ion
An atom with a charge, meaning it has a different number of protons and electrons.
Chemical Bonds
Attractive force between atoms when their electrons arise, forming a molecule. When a molecule has different types of atoms, it is called a compound. There are also multiple types of bonds.
1) Ionic Bond: Attraction of opposite forces (ions) forms a bond, where each side of the molecule is polar (slightly positive of negative).
2) Covalent Bond: Two atoms share a pair of electrons, filling each atom's vacancies partially. These can be stronger than ionic bonds.
3) Hydrogen Bond: Attraction between covalently bonded hydrogen and an electronegative (something with a strong pull on electrons) atom. Weakest type of bond.
Properties of Water
1) Cohesion: Water molecules do not easily separate because of hydrogen bonds. This is essential for many processes, such as evaporation and transpiration.
2) Stablizes Temperature: Hydrogen bonds keep temperature of water from rising too easily, which is essential for homeostasis.
3) Universal Solvent: Water easily dissolves other hydrophilic (water loving) solutes, but not hydrophobic (water hating) solutes such as oil.
Concentration
Amount of solute dissolved in a given volume. The measure of hydrogen ion concentration is pH. Substances that give off hydrogen ions when dissolved are called acids and have a low pH, while substances that accept hydrogen ions when dissolved are called bases and have a high pH.
Buffer
Chemicals that resist pH change and are essential for homeostasis.
Chapter 3: Molecules of Life
Click the black boxes to reveal the definitions.
Organic Molecule
A compound consisting of mostly hydrogen and carbon atoms.
Monomers
Small, organic molecules such as simple sugars, fatty acids, amino acids, and nucleotides, which are used to build larger molecules, called polymers.
Reactions
A process in which a molecule changes. Metabolism refers to reactions in which cells use energy, which requires enzymes (proteins that speed up reactions). Examples:
1) Condensation: Enzymes covalently bond two molecules together.
2) Hydrolysis: Large organic molecules being broken into smaller subunits.
Functional Groups
Clusters of atoms covalently bonded to a carbon atom of an organic molecule.
Carbohydrates
Organic compounds with a 1:2:1 ratio of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. These compounds are essential for fuel, structural materials, and storing energy. Types of carbohydrates:
1) Monosaccharide: Simple form of sugar.
2) Disaccharide: Two monosaccharides bound together.
3) Polysaccharide: Chains of saccharides. Common examples are cellulose (plant structural material), starch (energy storage in plants), and glycogen (energy storage in animals).
Lipids
Fatty, oily, or waxy organic compounds. There are many types, here are a few main ones:
1) Fats: Glycerol head, with one to three fatty acid tails. A triglyceride has three fatty acid tails, and it completely hydrophobic. If the hydrocarbon tail has any double bonds, it is considered unsaturated, but if it only has single bonds, it is saturated.
2) Phospholipids: Two fatty acid tails, with a phosphate group head. The heads are hydrophilic while the tails are hyrdrophobic. They are an essential part of lipid bilayers (cell membrane).
3) Wax: Fatty acid tails bind to a carbon ring/alcohol and is water repellent.
4) Steroids: Have four carbon rings but not fatty acid tails, some are hormones.
Fatty acids
Organic molecule with hydrocarbon tails (hydrophobic) and a carboxyl group head (hydrophilic).
Protein
Polymer of amino acids (held together by peptide bonds) with diverse functions. Its function is dictates by its structure.
Protein Structure
1) Primary Structure: Sequence of amino acids.
2) Secondary Structure: Polypeptide chain twists into a helix.
3) Tertiary Structure: Chain folds into a functional domain.
4) Quaternary Structure: Multiple chains combine to form a molecule.
Denature
Change in temperature, pH, salinity causes a protein to unravel, losing its funciton.
Prion
Infectous protein.
Nucleic Acids
Chains of nucleotides joined by sugar-phosphate bonds. DNA, RNA, and ATP are a few examples.
Chapter 4: Cell Strucutre and Function
Click the black boxes to reveal the definitions.
Components of All Cells
1) Plasma Membrane: Membrane that separates cell from its environment, made up of phospholipids and proteins. It controls what can enter and exit the cell.
2) Cytoplasm: Jellylike mixture of fluids within plasma membrane, holds various organelles.
3) DNA: Nucleic acid that holds hereditary information. In eukaryotes, it is held in the nucleus.
Surface to Volume Ratio
Cells are limited by this ratio. Surface area does not increases as quickly with size as volume does. Cell membranes can only handle so many interactions with its environment, so cells are limited in size.
Cell Theory
1) Living organisms have one or more cells.
2) A cell is the smallest unit of life, and is individually alive within an organism.
3) All cells arise from preexisting cells.
4) Cells contain hereditary information which they pass onto offspring.
Types of Microscopes
1) Light Microscopes: Uses light to illuminate samples.
2) Phase-contrast Microscopes: Uses dyes absorbed by transparent specimens.
3) Fluorescence Microscopes: Specimen emits light when a laser is focused onto it.
4) Electron Microscopes: Uses a focused beam of electrons to see extremely small specimens.
Characteristics of Prokaryotes
1) Ribosomes: Organelles that assemble polypeptides.
2) Plasmids: Small rings of DNA that carry some genes.
3) Nucleoid: Irregularly shaped region of cytoplasm.
4) Cell Wall: Semi-rigid wall that surrounds the plasma membrane.
5) Flagella: Long, slender strucures used to propel the cell.
6) Pili: Short protein filaments projecting from the surface that helps cell cling to surfaces.
Biofilm
Community of bacteria living collectively within a shared mass of slime.
Nucleus
Membrane that protects cell's DNA and controls passage of certain molecules across the membrane. The nuclear envelope is the external boundary of the nucleus, and contains pores for passage in and out of the nucleus. Inside the nucleus is the nucleolus, a dense region of proteins and nucleic acid where ribosome subunits are produced. Nucleoplasm, similar to the cytoplasm, suspends chromosomes (DNA molecules with proteins).
Endomembrane system
Interacting organelles whose goal is to make lipids, enzymes, proteins, and to destroy toxins and waste. Components:
1) Endoplasmic Reticulum: Rough ER (has ribosomes) makes polypeptides, while Smooth ER assembles lipids and breaks down carbs and toxins.
2) Vesicles: Sacs that transport substances between organelles or take part in digestion (lysosomes) or disposing toxins (peroxisomes). Plants have a large vesicle called the central vacuole.
3) Golgi Bodies: Organelle that puts finishing touches on proteins and lipids from the ER.
Mitochondrion
Eukaryotic organelle responsible for ATP production via respiration.
Plastids
Double membraned organelles in plants that are used in photosynthesis or storage. One example is the chloroplast, which is essential for photosynthesis.
Cytoskeleton
Interconnected protein filaments that reinforces, organizes, and moves the cell. Components:
1) Microtubules: Hollow cylinders made of tubulin that can assemble and disassemble quickly to form a scaffolding.
2) Microfilaments: Fibers made of actin that make up the cell cortex, a reinforcing mesh under the plasma membrane. These filaments can help move the cell.
3) Intermediate Filaments: Filaments that support various aspects of the cell.
4) Motor Proteins: Proteins that use ATP to transport various objects inside the cell as well as to move flagella and cilia.
5) Centriole: Organelle from which microtubules grow, develops from a basal body.
6) Pseudopods: Temporary extensions from cell that help with movement and engulfing prey.
Extracellular Matrix
Secretion of proteins and polysaccharides that serve various functions. One example is a cell wall. Another ECM is cuticle, a secretion that protects leaves with waxes.
Cell Junctions
Structures that connect cells to one another and the environment for easy signalling. Examples:
1) Plasmodesmata: Channels extending across cell walls in plants that allows for transport of water, nutrients, and signalling molecules.
2) Tight Junctions: In animals, rows of proteins that prevent fluids from leaking out (such as stomach lining).
3) Adhering Junctions: In animals, adhesion proteins that connect that are subject to stretching, such as skin cells.
4) Gap Junctions: In animals, channels that connect cytoplasms of adjacent cells, allowing ions and small molecules to pass.
